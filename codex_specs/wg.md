# Agent Identity and Trust Working Group Briefing

## The Challenge and Gap  
Modern identity protocols (OAuth, OIDC, etc.) were designed for human users logging into applications or for service-to-service API calls. They were **not built with autonomous agents in mind**, leaving a gap when AI agents act independently. As multiple agents collaborate or transact autonomously, we lack a standard way to answer the fundamental question: **“Who does this agent act on behalf of, and what is it allowed to do?”** In current systems, there is *no fast or reliable way to verify an agent’s origin, its owner, or the user’s intent behind it* [Know Your Agent (KYA): An Identity Framework for Agentic Commerce](https://www.trulioo.com/resources/white-papers/know-your-agent-an-identity-framework-for-trusted-agentic-commerce#:~:text=From%20cart,that%20blur%20traditional%20identity%20signals). Traditional identity signals are blurred when software agents initiate actions without direct user input. This uncertainty erodes trust and creates liability concerns, since it’s unclear **which human or organization the agent represents and what permissions it has been granted**.

## Proposed Mission: **Agent Passport** and Dynamic Trust  
**Agent Passport:** The working group’s mission could be to define a portable **“Agent Passport”** standard – a secure, transferable identity credential that **lets an agent prove who it is acting for and what authority it has** as it moves across the web. The concept (inspired by emerging “Know Your Agent” frameworks) is a **tamper-proof digital credential** stating *who built the agent, who it represents, and its permission scope* [Trulioo Bets on Digital Agent Passport to Keep Bots Honest](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2025/trulioo-bets-on-a-digital-agent-passport-to-keep-bots-honest-at-checkout/#:~:text=At%20the%20heart%20of%20the,and%20what%20permissions%20it%20has). Such an Agent Passport would attach rich metadata to an agent’s identity (e.g. owner, developer, capabilities, delegated rights) in a verifiable way. This enables any service or domain to quickly validate an agent’s **credentials and provenance** before trusting it.

**Dynamic Trust:** In addition, the WG should promote moving beyond static, one-time permissions toward **contextual, dynamic trust** models. An agent’s trust level and permissions might *adjust based on its real-time behavior and the risk profile of each task*. The Agent Passport framework can incorporate continuous validation checkpoints – for example, monitoring the agent’s behavior and risk signals and updating its trust score or access rights on the fly [Trulioo Bets on Digital Agent Passport to Keep Bots Honest](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2025/trulioo-bets-on-a-digital-agent-passport-to-keep-bots-honest-at-checkout/#:~:text=The%20passport%20framework%20includes%20five,rely%20on%20at%20machine%20speed) [Trulioo Bets on Digital Agent Passport to Keep Bots Honest](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2025/trulioo-bets-on-a-digital-agent-passport-to-keep-bots-honest-at-checkout/#:~:text=In%20practice%2C%20KYA%20would%20combine,be%20flagged%20or%20revoked%20instantly). This means if an agent deviates from expected behavior (e.g. tries an unauthorized action or operates in a high-risk manner), its “passport” can be flagged or revoked instantly. By **baking in behavior telemetry and continuous risk scoring** into the identity layer, trust becomes adaptive: low-risk routine tasks face minimal friction, whereas high-risk actions trigger stricter verification [Trulioo Bets on Digital Agent Passport to Keep Bots Honest](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2025/trulioo-bets-on-a-digital-agent-passport-to-keep-bots-honest-at-checkout/#:~:text=The%20passport%20framework%20includes%20five,rely%20on%20at%20machine%20speed). The goal is to ensure **agents are trustworthy, accountable, and operating within bounds at all times**, without relying solely on static pre-defined permissions.

## Key Scope Areas  

### 1. Delegation Protocols  
**Challenge:** Agents will often delegate tasks to other agents. If Agent A is authorized by a user and then hires Agent B to help, how do B’s permissions get derived and constrained? How do we propagate the chain of authority? Current OAuth delegation (“on-behalf-of” flows) only partially address this; they break down when the caller is an autonomous agent making independent decisions [Explaining OAuth Delegation, 'On Behalf Of', and Agent Identity for AI Agents](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/explaining-oauth-delegation-behalf-agent-identity-ai-agents-posta-wpiwc#:~:text=As%20I%20work%20with%20enterprise,when%20agents%20enter%20the%20picture) [Explaining OAuth Delegation, 'On Behalf Of', and Agent Identity for AI Agents](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/explaining-oauth-delegation-behalf-agent-identity-ai-agents-posta-wpiwc#:~:text=To%20support%20these%20requirements%2C%20we,and%20what%20caused%20the%20calls). We need a standard way to represent **transitive delegation of authority** among agents.  

**Approach:** Define protocols or token designs for **agent-to-agent delegation**. When Agent A delegates to Agent B, B should receive credentials that prove: (a) the original user or principal and Agent A have authorized this, and (b) the scope is limited to what A is permitted to delegate. For example, extending OAuth2 **token exchange** (RFC 8693) such that the new token carries both the user’s identity and the agent’s identity as an actor [Explaining OAuth Delegation, 'On Behalf Of', and Agent Identity for AI Agents](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/explaining-oauth-delegation-behalf-agent-identity-ai-agents-posta-wpiwc#:~:text=To%20support%20these%20requirements%2C%20we,and%20what%20caused%20the%20calls). This might involve **nested or chained identity claims** (to capture who authorized whom) and an “actor” field in tokens identifying the calling agent. The standard should ensure **permissions narrow at each delegation** – B cannot do more than what A was allowed, and accountability is maintained. Industry efforts are already exploring this: e.g. Microsoft Entra’s Agent ID uses on-behalf-of flows that **make the AI agent a first-class actor in the token** rather than an invisible intermediary [Explaining OAuth Delegation, 'On Behalf Of', and Agent Identity for AI Agents](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/explaining-oauth-delegation-behalf-agent-identity-ai-agents-posta-wpiwc#:~:text=One%20concrete%20example%20of%20agentic,it%20as%20an%20invisible%20intermediary). The WG can build on such ideas to recommend a uniform **delegation chain protocol** so that trust and authority flow downstream securely. This will answer *“If I trust Agent A, and A delegates to B, how do we securely extend trust to B?”* by cryptographically linking B’s credentials to A’s and the original principal’s identity.

### 2. Cross-Domain Identity  
**Challenge:** Agents frequently need to operate across organizational boundaries. For instance, an agent from a vendor company may need to authenticate and perform actions inside a client’s secure environment. Today, cross-domain trust is ad hoc – API keys or partner agreements – with no universal standard for recognizing an external agent’s identity and permissions. The question arises: *How can an agent from one domain be trusted in another domain’s context, without manual onboarding each time?*  

**Approach:** Develop a **federated identity framework for agents**. The Agent Passport should be usable across domains, much like a passport works across countries. This could involve **trusted third-party authorities** that vouch for an agent’s identity. For example, the WG might specify **“Digital Agent Passport Authorities”** (analogous to certificate authorities) that can issue, sign, and verify agent passports [Trulioo Bets on Digital Agent Passport to Keep Bots Honest](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2025/trulioo-bets-on-a-digital-agent-passport-to-keep-bots-honest-at-checkout/#:~:text=A%20critical%20part%20of%20the,lists%20current%20in%20real%20time). Each agent would carry a passport signed by a known authority (which could be an identity provider, industry consortium, or network). When an agent from Vendor wants access in Client’s domain, the client’s systems can verify the passport’s signature and check it against a **federated whitelist or directory** of trusted agents [Trulioo Bets on Digital Agent Passport to Keep Bots Honest](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2025/trulioo-bets-on-a-digital-agent-passport-to-keep-bots-honest-at-checkout/#:~:text=A%20critical%20part%20of%20the,lists%20current%20in%20real%20time). The standard should outline how to construct these identity chains across organizational boundaries – possibly leveraging existing federated identity tech (SAML/OIDC federation, PKI, etc.) but tailored for autonomous agents. By establishing a **common trust registry or directory of agent identities**, any domain can authenticate an external agent and ascertain “who is sponsoring this agent and what it’s allowed to do” with confidence. Ensuring interoperability (so that an agent passport issued in one ecosystem is recognized in another) will be key. The WG can coordinate with existing standards bodies (e.g. W3C, OIDC) to incorporate agent identity claims that transcend siloed identity domains. Ultimately, an agent from a vendor should be able to prove to a client’s system: “Here is my passport. It’s issued by a trusted authority and confirms I represent User X from Company Y with roles Z.”

### 3. Auditability and Accountability  
**Challenge:** With autonomous agents acting on behalf of users or organizations, **liability and compliance** become critical. If something goes wrong – say an agent makes an unauthorized purchase or a poor decision – we need a clear audit trail of “who decided what.” This involves two concepts: the **chain of thought** (the internal reasoning an agent used) and the **chain of command** (the hierarchy of authority: e.g. user -> Agent A -> Agent B). Traditional logs often only show that *“User X’s credentials were used to do Y”*, which is insufficient when an AI agent actually initiated the action. We must distinguish human actions from AI agent actions and provide traceability for every agent decision [Explaining OAuth Delegation, 'On Behalf Of', and Agent Identity for AI Agents](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/explaining-oauth-delegation-behalf-agent-identity-ai-agents-posta-wpiwc#:~:text=%2A%20compliance%20and%20audit%20,to%20authorize%20for%20capabilities%20not). 

**Approach:** The WG should define **standards for auditing agent-led actions**. Every transaction or operation an agent performs could carry metadata (in headers or logs) identifying **which agent (and version of its code) initiated it, on behalf of which user, under whose authorization**. The Agent Passport can include a unique agent ID and perhaps an audit endpoint. We may require that agents maintain an event log of their decisions (a “chain of thought” record) that can be inspected when needed for compliance. At minimum, systems receiving requests from agents should log an indicator that “this request was made by *Agent123 acting for UserXYZ*” to differentiate from direct user actions [Explaining OAuth Delegation, 'On Behalf Of', and Agent Identity for AI Agents](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/explaining-oauth-delegation-behalf-agent-identity-ai-agents-posta-wpiwc#:~:text=%2A%20compliance%20and%20audit%20,to%20authorize%20for%20capabilities%20not). The working group can explore standardizing an **agent identity claim in access tokens or API calls** (for example, a field that carries the agent’s ID and maybe parent agent ID if delegated) to enable end-to-end traceability. Additionally, guidelines for **behavior logging** could be set (e.g. recording the prompts an agent was given, the options it considered) to the extent feasible, in order to reconstruct the agent’s decision process if needed. Ensuring auditability will support liability determinations: organizations can trace whether a bad outcome was due to the agent’s autonomous choice, a user instruction, or a delegated sub-agent action. By establishing a **“chain of command” record in the identity and authorization flow**, we can provide clear answers to *who authorized an action and who (which agent) actually decided to execute it*, which is essential for accountability [Explaining OAuth Delegation, 'On Behalf Of', and Agent Identity for AI Agents](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/explaining-oauth-delegation-behalf-agent-identity-ai-agents-posta-wpiwc#:~:text=To%20support%20these%20requirements%2C%20we,and%20what%20caused%20the%20calls). These audit trails should be tamper-evident and possibly governed by the same passport authorities (for example, signing agent logs). The end result is improved confidence that any autonomous agent action can be transparently traced back through the permissions and decisions that led to it.

## Working Group To-Do List  
To proceed, the Working Group should accomplish the following steps:

- **Establish Meeting Cadence:** Decide on a regular meeting schedule (e.g. weekly or bi-weekly conference calls, a consistent day/time) to maintain momentum.  
- **Finalize Mission and Scope:** Refine and formally agree on the WG’s mission statement and the key focus areas (like those above). Ensure everyone aligns on what problems will be tackled and what is out of scope.  
- **Define Deliverables:** Determine the final output of the WG’s efforts. This could be a whitepaper summarizing standards recommendations, a draft specification for an “Agent Passport” protocol, reference implementations, etc. Clarifying this will guide the group's work plan.  
- **Develop a WG Charter:** Create a charter document that outlines the WG’s purpose, scope, deliverables, and timeline. This charter should be submitted to the Governing Board for review and approval, per the organization’s process. The charter will serve as a formal blueprint for the WG’s activities and goals.  
- **Assign Leadership Roles:** Appoint the Working Group chair and co-chair(s). (According to policy, these leadership positions are typically open only to Platinum & Gold member representatives.) Ensuring strong leadership will help drive the project forward, so selecting experienced individuals from the member organizations is crucial.  

By addressing the challenge of agent identity through the **Agent Passport** framework, instituting dynamic and contextual trust controls, and covering delegation, cross-domain trust, and auditability, the Working Group can pave the way for **secure and accountable agent-based systems**. This briefing outlined both high-level vision and specific tactical steps. With a clear mission and collaborative effort, the group will be positioned to deliver guidelines or standards that answer the question, “Who does this agent act on behalf of, and what is it allowed to do?” – thereby closing the current gap in identity protocols for autonomous agents and fostering trust in agent-driven commerce and interactions.
